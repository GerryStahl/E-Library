## Theoretical Evolution: From Post-Cognitive Revolution to Mechanistic Integration

The field's foundational commitment was established immediately: CSCL studies intersubjective meaning-making at the group level, rejecting individualistic frameworks inherited from educational psychology. The 2006 editorials positioned collaborative learning not as individuals working together but as joint meaning-making irreducible to aggregated individual cognition—a "post-cognitive paradigm" that would anchor the field throughout its history.

Early years (2006–2010) focused on articulating what made CSCL distinctive. The crucial theoretical construct was intersubjectivity—not similar individual beliefs but productive participation in joint discourse within a shared world. By 2015, this crystallized into CSCL's defining characteristic: groups achieve intersubjectivity when members interact well enough to pursue collective aims, making collaborative learning fundamentally a group-level phenomenon.

The persistent challenge was connecting multiple levels of analysis—individual learning, small-group interaction, and community knowledge-building. The 2008 SWISH principle (Split When Interaction Should Happen) represented early attempts to design across levels. By 2013, the field proposed "interactional resources" as boundary objects bridging levels through progressive development from reified to sedimented to institutionalized forms. The 2018 framework of "coupling minds" at different cognitive levels—attention, core cognition, metacognition, and activities—represented the decade's most comprehensive integration attempt.

A critical development emerged in the second decade: integration of emotional and motivational dimensions previously underemphasized. The 2016 leadership transition explicitly called for connecting social, emotional, and cognitive aspects. By 2022, the field had incorporated group awareness, self-regulation, and emotional dynamics into comprehensive models of collaborative learning, acknowledging that productive collaboration requires managing multiple simultaneously operating dimensions.

The culminating theoretical development came in 2025 with the proposal of mechanistic explanation as a unifying construct. Rather than seeking theoretical convergence, this framework distinguished causal-probabilistic models from mechanistic explanations describing how causes produce effects through continuous organized activities. This approach could accommodate cognitive and sociocultural perspectives within a common analytical framework—addressing the tension between these traditions that had persisted throughout the field's history.
