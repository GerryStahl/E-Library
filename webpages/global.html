Volume 15. Global Introduction to CSCL

overview

This book is a deliberate attempt to reach a global audience for the foundational ideas of computer-supported collaborative learning. Its anchor is the 2020 third English edition of the CSCL introduction, presenting the field's core argument that effective online learning requires collaborative knowledge building, intersubjective meaning making, and technologies specifically designed to support group cognition—not merely the digitization of traditional instruction. Surrounding that central text are the 2006 original English version and six translations into Spanish, Portuguese, Simplified Chinese, Traditional Chinese, Romanian, and German. The book makes available to researchers and educators worldwide, in their own languages, the theoretical and methodological foundations of a field whose urgency became undeniable during the global pandemic.

English third edition (2020)

Chapter 1: English third edition (2020)

This chapter—co-authored with Timothy Koschmann and Dan Suthers and revised in 2020 amid the Coronavirus pandemic—serves as the foundational introduction to the field of computer-supported collaborative learning (CSCL). Its central argument is that the sudden worldwide turn to online education during the pandemic exposed a critical gap: the communication tools and course management platforms teachers hastily adopted were never designed to support learning. CSCL, by contrast, is built specifically to support collaborative knowledge building, dialogic interaction, intersubjective meaning making, and group cognition.

The chapter locates CSCL within a historical sequence of educational computing approaches—from behaviorist computer-assisted instruction through cognitivist intelligent tutoring systems and constructivist programming pedagogy—arguing that CSCL represents a fourth and qualitatively different paradigm, one grounded in social-constructivist and dialogical theories rather than in models of individual cognition. A pivotal distinction is drawn between cooperative learning (individuals divide tasks and assemble results) and collaborative learning (participants do the work together, constructing shared meaning through interaction). Collaborative learning, the chapter argues, is not reducible to the sum of individual learning and therefore cannot be adequately studied with traditional pre/post-test methods.

Epistemologically, the chapter contrasts a "Correspondence Theory of Truth"—underlying traditional educational research, in which knowledge consists of propositions matching a fixed reality—with a "Coherence Theory of Truth" appropriate to collaborative settings, in which what counts as knowledge is worked out within interaction and is inextricably bound to context. This shift calls for new methodologies: conversation analysis, video analysis, and ethnomethodologically informed case studies that trace how groups intersubjectively construct shared meaning, rather than coding and counting behavioral variables.

Three research methodologies are surveyed—experimental studies, descriptive case studies, and design-based research—and the chapter argues for hybrid approaches that combine their strengths while respecting their distinct epistemological commitments. It closes by identifying the small group as the most productive unit of analysis for CSCL research: large enough for the full range of social interaction, small enough for shared meaning making to be visible; and positioned as the mediating level between individual learners and the broader learning community.
